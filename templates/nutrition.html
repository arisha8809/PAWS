Essential Nutrients

Understanding the key nutritional elements is crucial for your dog's health and longevity. Hover over each nutrient to learn more.

Proteins

Building blocks for muscles, organs, and immune system. Dogs need animal-based proteins like chicken, beef, and fish.

Fats

Provides energy and supports brain health. Look for omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids for a shiny coat.

Carbohydrates

Energy source found in grains, vegetables, and legumes. Provides fiber for digestive health.

Vitamins & Minerals

Essential for metabolism, bone growth, and immune function. A balanced diet should provide all necessary micronutrients.

Hydration

Clean water is essential for digestion, temperature regulation, and organ function. Always provide fresh water.

      Puppy Nutrition (0-12 months)

      Puppies need nutrient-dense food to support their rapid growth and development. Their diet should be higher in protein, fat, and certain minerals compared to adult dogs.

      üß† Brain Development

      Look for DHA (docosahexaenoic acid) which supports brain and vision development during the critical first year.

      ü¶¥ Bone Growth

      Proper calcium-to-phosphorus ratio is essential for healthy bone development. Too much calcium can be harmful, especially for large breeds.

      Adult Nutrition (1-7 years)

      Adult dogs need balanced nutrition to maintain their health and energy levels. Their metabolic rate stabilizes, so portion control becomes important to prevent weight gain.

      ‚öñÔ∏è Weight Management

      Monitor your dog's body condition regularly and adjust portions to maintain ideal weight. Obesity can lead to numerous health problems.

      üèÉ Activity Matching

      Match calorie intake to activity level. Working and sporting dogs need more calories than less active companions.

      ü¶∑ Dental Health

      Consider dental health when choosing food. Dry kibble and dental chews can help reduce plaque buildup.

      Senior Nutrition (8+ years)

      Senior dogs often have decreased energy requirements but need high-quality, easily digestible nutrients to support aging body systems and manage chronic conditions.

      üîÑ Metabolic Changes

      Senior dogs typically need fewer calories but higher quality protein to maintain muscle mass as they age.

      üåø Joint Support

      Look for foods with glucosamine, chondroitin, and omega-3 fatty acids to support joint health and mobility.

      ü´Ä Heart Health

      Consider foods with lower sodium levels and L-carnitine to support heart function in aging dogs.

      ‚ö†Ô∏è Foods to Avoid

      Some human foods can be toxic to dogs. Never feed your dog: chocolate, grapes/raisins, onions, garlic, xylitol (found in sugar-free products), alcohol, caffeine, macadamia nuts, or raw dough.

      Always research before sharing human food with your pet, and consult your veterinarian if you suspect your dog has consumed something toxic.

      How often should I feed my adult dog? +

      Most adult dogs do well with two meals per day, spaced about 12 hours apart. Some very active dogs may benefit from an additional small meal. Consistent feeding times help with digestion and house training. Avoid free-feeding (leaving food out all day) as it can lead to obesity and makes it harder to monitor food intake.

      Should I feed my dog raw food? +

      Raw diets are controversial. While some owners report benefits like improved coat and energy levels, raw diets carry risks of bacterial contamination (E. coli, Salmonella) that can affect both dogs and humans in the household. If you choose a raw diet, work with a veterinary nutritionist to ensure it's nutritionally complete and follow strict food safety protocols.

      How can I tell if my dog is at a healthy weight? +

      You should be able to feel your dog's ribs without pressing hard, but they shouldn't be prominently visible. Looking from above, your dog should have a visible waist. From the side, the abdomen should tuck up from the ribcage to the hindquarters. If you can't feel the ribs or there's no waist definition, your dog may be overweight. Consult your vet for personalized weight management advice.

      Are supplements necessary for my dog? +

      A high-quality, complete and balanced commercial dog food should provide all the nutrients your dog needs. Unnecessary supplementation can cause imbalances or toxicity. However, certain conditions or life stages might benefit from specific supplements as recommended by your veterinarian. Always consult your vet before adding any supplements to your dog's diet.
